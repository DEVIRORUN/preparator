SECTION A: DC MACHINES
Question 1: Theory Solutions
● a) Separately vs. Self-Excited: In a separately excited generator, the field winding is
energized by an independent external DC power source. In a self-excited generator, the
field winding is powered directly by the electricity generated by its own armature. For a
self-excited generator to start up, the field poles must possess Residual Magnetism (a
small permanent magnetic field left over from previous operations) to induce an initial
small voltage when the shaft begins spinning.
● b) Critical Field Resistance: It is the maximum field circuit resistance above which a
self-excited DC generator will fail to excite or build up any voltage. If your field circuit
resistance exceeds this value, the current flowing through the field coils is too low to
reinforce the residual magnetism, and the generator output remains near zero.
● c) Inrush Current: At the exact instant a motor is turned on, its rotational speed ($N$) is
exactly zero. Because Back EMF ($E$) is directly proportional to speed ($E \propto N$),
the Back EMF at startup is 0 V. With no Back EMF to oppose the supply voltage, the
starting current is limited only by the tiny resistance of the armature winding ($I_a =
\frac{V - 0}{R_a}$), creating a massive current spike.
Question 2: Calculation Solutions
Given: $P = 4$, Wave-Wound $\implies$ Parallel paths ($C$) = 2, $I_L = 50\text{ A}$, $V =
300\text{ V}$, $R_a = 0.2~\Omega$, $R_f = 150~\Omega$, $Z = 500$, $\Phi = 0.02\text{ Wb}$.
● a) Shunt Field Current ($I_f$):
$$I_f = \frac{V}{R_f} = \frac{300}{150} = \mathbf{2\text{ A}}$$
● b) Total Armature Current ($I_a$):
For a shunt generator:
$$I_a = I_L + I_f = 50 + 2 = \mathbf{52\text{ A}}$$
● c) Generated EMF ($E$):
$$E = V + I_aR_a = 300 + (52 \times 0.2) = 300 + 10.4 = \mathbf{310.4\text{ V}}$$
● d) Operational Speed ($N$):
Using the general EMF formula: $E = \frac{Z \cdot N \cdot P \cdot \Phi}{60 \cdot C}$
Rearranging specifically for speed ($N$):
$$N = \frac{E \times 60 \times C}{Z \times P \times \Phi}$$
Substitute values ($C = 2$ for wave wound):
$$N = \frac{310.4 \times 60 \times 2}{500 \times 4 \times 0.02}$$
$$N = \frac{37248}{40} = \mathbf{931.2\text{ rpm}}$$
SECTION B: TRANSFORMERS
Question 3: Theory Solutions
● a) Maximum Efficiency Condition: A transformer achieves its absolute highest
efficiency when its variable losses equal its constant losses. Mathematically:
$$\text{Copper Loss } (P_{Cu}) = \text{Iron Loss } (P_{Fe})$$
● b) Material Design Logic: Silicon is added to steel to increase its electrical resistivity,
which dramatically reduces Eddy Current heat losses, while preserving high magnetic
permeability. Core laminations are insulated from one another to interrupt the physical
paths of swirling eddy currents, stopping them from circulating through the bulk of the
metal core.
● c) Voltage Regulation: It is the percentage change in secondary terminal voltage when
full load is dropped completely to zero. A lower percentage value is highly preferred
because it indicates that the secondary voltage remains stable and constant whether the
consumer load is light or heavy.
Question 4: Calculation Solutions
Given: $S = 10\text{ kVA} = 10,000\text{ VA}$, $V_p/V_s = 400/200\text{ V}$, $P_{Fe} = 150\text{
W}$, $P_{Cu(FL)} = 300\text{ W}$, $\text{pf} = 1.0$.
● a) Full Load Efficiency ($\eta_{FL}$):
$$\text{Output Power} = 10,000 \times 1.0 = 10,000\text{ W}$$
$$\text{Total Losses} = P_{Fe} + P_{Cu(FL)} = 150 + 300 = 450\text{ W}$$
$$\eta_{FL} = \frac{10000}{10000 + 450} \times 100\% = \frac{10000}{10450} \times
100\% = \mathbf{95.69\%}$$
● b) Fractional Load for Maximum Efficiency ($x$):
$$x = \sqrt{\frac{P_{Fe}}{P_{Cu(FL)}}} = \sqrt{\frac{150}{300}} = \sqrt{0.5} \approx
\mathbf{0.7071}$$
(Maximum efficiency occurs at precisely 70.71% of full load)
● c) Maximum Efficiency Value ($\eta_{max}$):
At this fractional load, the new Output Power is:
$$\text{Output} = x \times S \times \text{pf} = 0.7071 \times 10,000 \times 1.0 = 7071\text{
W}$$
At maximum efficiency, copper loss equals iron loss ($150\text{ W}$), so total losses are
exactly doubled iron loss:
$$\text{Total Losses} = 2 \times P_{Fe} = 2 \times 150 = 300\text{ W}$$
$$\eta_{max} = \frac{7071}{7071 + 300} \times 100\% = \frac{7071}{7371} \times 100\% =
\mathbf{95.93\%}$$
SECTION C: COMMUNICATION SYSTEMS
Question 5: Theory Solutions
● a) Sidebands: When a high-frequency carrier ($f_c$) is modulated by a lower-frequency
baseband signal ($f_m$), new frequencies are generated above and below the carrier.
These are sidebands: the Upper Sideband ($\text{USB} = f_c + f_m$) and the Lower
Sideband ($\text{LSB} = f_c - f_m$). They contain the actual information being
transmitted.
● b) Bandwidth Differences: Standard AM transmits both sidebands simultaneously
($\text{USB} + \text{LSB}$), requiring a total bandwidth equal to twice the highest
modulating frequency ($\text{BW} = 2 \cdot f_m$). Single Sideband (SSB) engineering
filters out one sideband and the carrier completely, saving spectrum space and requiring
only half the bandwidth ($\text{BW} = f_m$).
● c) Noise Immunity (AM vs. FM): FM is highly superior to AM in noise immunity.
Atmospheric noise and lightning spikes naturally alter the amplitude (height) of an
incoming radio wave. Because AM relies entirely on height variations to extract audio
data, noise directly corrupts the signal. FM relies strictly on frequency shifts; receivers
use limiters to clip off amplitude noise completely without damaging the underlying data.
Question 6: Calculations
Given: $V_{max} = 12\text{ V}$, $V_{min} = 4\text{ V}$.
● a) Carrier Amplitude ($A_c$):
$$A_c = \frac{V_{max} + V_{min}}{2} = \frac{12 + 4}{2} = \frac{16}{2} = \mathbf{8\text{ V}}$$
● b) Modulating Signal Amplitude ($A_m$):
$$A_m = \frac{V_{max} - V_{min}}{2} = \frac{12 - 4}{2} = \frac{8}{2} = \mathbf{4\text{ V}}$$
● c) Modulation Index ($m$) and Percentage:
$$m = \frac{A_m}{A_c} = \frac{4}{8} = \mathbf{0.5}$$
$$\text{Percentage Modulation} = 0.5 \times 100\% = \mathbf{50\%}$$
SECTION D: SEMICONDUCTORS & BJT
Question 7: Theory Solutions
● a) Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic: An intrinsic semiconductor is completely pure, natural
crystalline material (like pure silicon) with low conductivity at room temperature. An
extrinsic semiconductor is a pure material that has undergone intentional chemical
doping to modify and exponentially increase its electrical conduction capacity.
● b) Barrier Potential: It is the internal electrical potential difference established across a
PN junction's depletion region that blocks further spontaneous diffusion of charge
carriers. At room temperature:
○ Silicon (Si) $\approx 0.7\text{ V}$
○ Germanium (Ge) $\approx 0.3\text{ V}$
● c) Base Region Geometry: The base layer must be exceptionally thin and lightly doped
so that the vast majority (95% to 99%) of charge carriers injected from the Emitter do not
recombine within the Base. Instead, they sweep right across the junction into the
Collector, ensuring high current gain ($\beta$).
Question 8: Calculations
Given: $\alpha = 0.985$, $I_B = 20\text{ μA} = 0.02\text{ mA}$.
● a) Common-Emitter Current Gain ($\beta$):
$$\beta = \frac{\alpha}{1 - \alpha}$$
$$\beta = \frac{0.985}{1 - 0.985} = \frac{0.985}{0.015} = \mathbf{65.67}$$
● b) Collector Current ($I_C$):
$$I_C = \beta \times I_B$$
$$I_C = 65.67 \times 0.02\text{ mA} = \mathbf{1.313\text{ mA}}$$
● c) Total Emitter Current ($I_E$):
$$I_E = I_C + I_B$$
$$I_E = 1.313\text{ mA} + 0.02\text{ mA} = \mathbf{1.333\text{ mA}}$$
(Verification using alpha: $I_E = \frac{I_C}{\alpha} = \frac{1.3134}{0.985} = 1.333\text{ mA}$.
Math balances perfectly!)'

SECTION A: DC MACHINES (MOTORS & GENERATORS)
Question 1: Theory Solutions
● a) Energy Conversion: A DC Generator converts mechanical energy (rotational force on
a shaft) into electrical energy (voltage/current). A DC Motor does the exact opposite,
converting electrical energy into mechanical energy.
● b) Commutator Function: In a generator, it acts as a mechanical rectifier, converting the
alternating current (AC) induced in the armature coils into direct current (DC) for the
external circuit. In a motor, it reverses the current direction in the armature coils every
half rotation, ensuring the torque continues pushing the shaft in one continuous
direction.
● c) Back EMF: It is the voltage generated inside a spinning motor that directly opposes
the supply voltage. It acts as a self-regulator because it limits the amount of current the
motor draws; if a heavy load slows the motor down, the Back EMF drops, which allows
more current to flow in and generate the extra torque needed to carry the load.
Question 2: Calculation Solutions
Given: $V = 440\text{ V}$, $I_a = 60\text{ A}$, $R_a = 0.25~\Omega$, Poles ($P$) = 6,
Conductors ($Z$) = 864, Flux ($\Phi$) = $0.04\text{ Wb}$.
Note on Winding: Lap wound means parallel paths ($C$) equal the number of poles ($P$).
Therefore, $C = 6$.
● a) Back EMF ($E$):
$E = V - I_aR_a$
$E = 440 - (60 \times 0.25)$
$E = 440 - 15$
$E = 425\text{ V}$
● b) Speed in rpm ($N$):
Using the EMF equation: $E = \frac{Z \cdot N \cdot P \cdot \Phi}{60 \cdot C}$
Since $C = P$ for lap winding, they cancel out: $E = \frac{Z \cdot N \cdot \Phi}{60}$
Rearranging for $N$:
$N = \frac{E \cdot 60}{Z \cdot \Phi}$
$N = \frac{425 \times 60}{864 \times 0.04}$
$N = \frac{25500}{34.56}$
$N = 737.85\text{ rpm}$
● c) Gross Mechanical Power ($P_m$):
$P_m = E \cdot I_a$
$P_m = 425 \times 60$
$P_m = 25,500\text{ W}$ (or 25.5 kW)
● d) Gross Torque ($T$):
$T = \frac{60 \cdot P_m}{2\pi \cdot N}$
$T = \frac{60 \times 25500}{2\pi \times 737.85}$
$T = \frac{1530000}{4636.05}$
$T = 330.02\text{ Nm}$
SECTION B: TRANSFORMERS
Question 3: Theory Solutions
● a) AC vs. DC Operation: Transformers rely on Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic
Induction. This requires a constantly changing magnetic field to induce a voltage across
the coils. AC voltage naturally fluctuates, creating this changing field. DC provides a flat,
constant current, resulting in a static magnetic field, meaning zero voltage is induced and
the transformer simply acts as a short circuit, drawing massive current and burning up.
● b) Core/Iron Losses:
○ Hysteresis Loss: The energy wasted forcing the magnetic domains in the core
material to constantly reverse direction with the AC cycle.
○ Eddy Current Loss: Unwanted, swirling electric currents induced inside the
physical iron core itself, creating heat. It is minimized by constructing the core out
of thin, insulated silicon steel sheets (laminations) rather than a solid block of metal.
● c) Ideal Transformer Characteristics:
1. Zero winding resistance (no copper loss).
2. Infinite core permeability (requires zero magnetizing current).
3. No leakage flux (100% of the magnetic field links primary to secondary).
4. 100% efficiency (Input Power = Output Power).
Question 4: Calculation Solutions
Given: Rating = $50\text{ kVA}$, $V_p/V_s = 2400/240\text{ V}$, $P_{Fe} = 400\text{ W}$,
$P_{Cu(FL)} = 600\text{ W}$, pf = 0.8.
● a) Secondary Full-Load Current ($I_s$):
$I_s = \frac{kVA \times 1000}{V_s}$
$I_s = \frac{50 \times 1000}{240}$
$I_s = 208.33\text{ A}$
● b) Efficiency at Full Load:
Output Power = $kVA \times 1000 \times pf = 50,000 \times 0.8 = 40,000\text{ W}$
Total Losses = $P_{Fe} + P_{Cu(FL)} = 400 + 600 = 1,000\text{ W}$
$\eta = \frac{Output}{Output + Losses} \times 100\%$
$\eta = \frac{40000}{40000 + 1000} \times 100\%$
$\eta = \frac{40000}{41000} \times 100\%$
$\eta = 97.56\%$
● c) Efficiency at Half Load:
Output Power = $0.5 \times 50,000 \times 0.8 = 20,000\text{ W}$
Half-Load Cu Loss = $(0.5)^2 \times P_{Cu(FL)} = 0.25 \times 600 = 150\text{ W}$
Total Losses = $P_{Fe} + P_{Cu(HL)} = 400 + 150 = 550\text{ W}$
$\eta = \frac{Output}{Output + Losses} \times 100\%$
$\eta = \frac{20000}{20000 + 550} \times 100\%$
$\eta = \frac{20000}{20550} \times 100\%$
$\eta = 97.32\%$

● b) Modulation: The process of superimposing a low-frequency, baseband information

● c) Reasons for Modulation:

2. Prevents interference by allowing different signals to use different carrier

3. Vastly increases the transmission range of the signal.
4. Improves signal quality and reduces the impact of noise.
● d) Frequency Band Matching:
○ VLF: Submarine/military communication.
○ MF: AM Radio broadcasting.
○ VHF: FM Radio and TV broadcasting.
○ SHF: Satellite TV (DStv) and radar.

SECTION D: SEMICONDUCTORS & BJT
Question 7: Theory Solutions
● a) Doping: The deliberate addition of specific impurity atoms into a pure (intrinsic)
semiconductor crystal lattice to increase and control its electrical conductivity.
● b) N-Type vs P-Type:
○ N-Type: Doped with pentavalent (5 valence electron) atoms. The majority charge
carriers are negatively charged electrons.
○ P-Type: Doped with trivalent (3 valence electron) atoms. The majority charge
carriers are positively charged holes.
● c) Depletion Region & Forward Bias: When P and N materials are joined, electrons from
the N-side diffuse across the junction to fill holes on the P-side. This leaves behind
immobile, charged ions that create a barrier region entirely depleted of free charge
carriers. When Forward Biased, the external voltage pushes majority carriers back into
the junction, narrowing the depletion region and overcoming the barrier potential so
current can flow.
Question 8: Structure & Calculation Solutions
● a) Schematic Symbols:
○ NPN: The arrow on the Emitter terminal points OUTWARD (away from the base).
"Not Pointing iN".
○ PNP: The arrow on the Emitter terminal points INWARD (toward the base).
● b) Current Gain Calculations:

$I_E = I_B + I_C$
